Abstract—second best developing-economy trade of last century has most certainly been Singapore 1965-1990. We analyze the factors that made it (and other economies like it) succeed, and apply it to today’s world. We discover results that seem strikingly surprising at first (Rwanda is in top-10. Yes, Rwanda); until one recalls that best investments often poses contrarian properties. In the course of our inquery, we show that country matching last century’s successes the best today, is Vietnam. And finally, we construct the portfolio allocation strategy, to maximize the value of that knowledge.

Index Terms—fundraising, investment, expected value

I.Thesis and approach

The second-best developing-economy trade of the last century was Singapore, 1965–1990: a poor, resource-less, newly-expelled island that compounded into a high-income economy in a single generation. The first-best is debatable; that Singapore is on the shortlist is not. It was not a fluke. It belongs to a small family of takeoffs — Korea and Taiwan from 1962, Singapore from 1965, China from 1980, Ireland from 1990, Poland from 1992 — each a generational compounding event in which a poor economy converged on the frontier and stayed converging. The convergence trade is the act of identifying such an economy before it runs, and holding it through the run.

The question this study answers is its empirical generalization: which developing economy today is configured the way the historical takeoffs were configured roughly five years before they took off?

The work proceeds in three movements. First (Section 3) we establish, against a labeled panel of historical takeoffs and a matched set of look-alike non-takeoffs, what actually predicted a takeoff five years out — separating the factors that lead an ascent from the ones that merely confirm one already happened. Second (Section 4) we keep only the factors that survive that test, each having cleared explicit out-of-sample bars. Third (Section 6, Section 7) we score an unprejudiced field of today’s developing economies against the survivors and report where the data lands. And lastly, we construct the portfolio to best express the bias.

II.Predictive Factors

Before measuring anything, one structural point decides what is even worth measuring, - and it follows from the definition of the trade itself.

The factors we screen on must be ones that are already visible in a country that has not yet converged. That single requirement splits every plausible national strength into two classes by where it sits in the causal chain of an ascent, and the chain has a forced direction. A country gets rich by first being able to make things the world wants more cheaply than incumbents; that capability has prerequisites and it has consequences, and the two cannot be reordered. The prerequisites are a skilled population, institutions clean and predictable enough that outside capital will commit on a multi-decade horizon, and enforceable contracts, - these have to exist first, because nothing downstream forms without them. The consequences: a large economy, a deep financial center, eventually a currency the world wants to hold — are produced by a rise that has already happened. They are the residue of success, not its cause.

A high reading on any prerequisite in a still-poor country is a genuine forward signal: the machinery that produces an ascent is in place and has not yet fired. A high reading on a consequence tells you only that a country already rose. Current GDP level, share of world output, financial-center depth, reserve-currency usage, and standing military are all consequences. They are lagging, thus we ignore them.

Notice our model lands close to a structure Ray Dalio arrives at. That convergence is convenient, as where our own labeled cases (Section 3) cover only the post-war takeoffs; his sequence chart (Fig. 1) lets us check the ordering against the rises and falls of earlier centuries — and the lead/lag pattern holds there too, which is reassurance that what we derived is structural rather than an artifact of a handful of modern cases.

The ordering tells us which class of factor to screen on, - the prerequisites. It does not tell us which prerequisites actually separate a country that took off from a look-alike that stalled with the same apparent strengths, nor how much each one matters. Those questions have only one honest answer: test them against the cases. That is the next section.

III.The empirical test

The spine of this study is a labeled-prediction test, not a reading of history.

Ground truth. We assemble a positive set of seven historical takeoffs: Korea 1962, Taiwan 1962, Singapore 1965, China 1980, Ireland 1990, Poland 1992, with Vietnam 1990 carried as an in-sample sanity check only; never as validation, since the thesis predicts Vietnam and using it to validate would be circular. A takeoff is defined formally, not impressionistically: the start year of a sustained convergence run of at least 4% real GDP-per-capita CAGR held for at least fifteen years, closing at least twenty percentage points of the gap to the US frontier. A country either meets this bar in the data or it does not.

The control set. Prediction is meaningless without the cases that look the part and fail. We match, over the same calendar windows, a panel of low- and lower-middle-income economies that did not take off: the Philippines of the 1960s, much of postwar Latin America, Sub-Saharan peers. Each candidate factor is judged not on whether it correlates with growth in general, but on whether it separates the takeoffs from their look-alike non-takeoffs.

This is the discipline that makes the test about prediction rather than coincidence, and it is exactly what privileges the leading factors over the coincident and lagging ones: a factor that only lights up at takeoff scores no better than chance five years out.

One mechanical objection has to be answered up front: the modern indicator series (World Bank WDI, Transparency International CPI, World Bank WGI) postdate the 1962–65 Asian takeoffs, so how does one score Korea in 1957? We splice longer-running proxies — Barro-Lee educational attainment, V-Dem’s rule-of-law index back to the eighteenth century, the Penn World Table for investment and total-factor-productivity — each calibrated to the modern series on their period of overlap. The calibration mechanics are deferred to Section 10; without them the most important templates would simply be unscoreable, and a test that quietly drops its hardest cases is not a test.

IV.From candidates to validated factors

Run through that test, the field of plausible prerequisites collapses to eleven factors that survive. They are not a scorecard reverse-engineered from one country; they are the determinants that demonstrably separated seven takeoffs from their look-alikes, out of sample.

The bar each factor cleared. To be retained, a factor had to pass all three of: a leave-one-out hit-rate of at least 70% (drop each takeoff, refit the threshold on the remaining six, re-predict the held-out one); a ROC-AUC of at least 0.70 separating takeoffs from the control set; and a Spearman rank-correlation of at least 0.40 between the factor’s pre-takeoff reading and the subsequent twenty-year growth outcome. A factor failing all three is dropped outright. A factor that passes AUC but fails the hit-rate is demoted to a modifier — never promoted to a gate.

Gates and fuel. The survivors fall into two kinds. Four are leading institutional gates, entering the composite multiplicatively — a near-floor reading on any one caps the whole composite no matter how strong the rest:

  1. Education / human-capital quality — the earliest-leading prerequisite of all; learning outcomes, not merely enrollment. (Threshold detail in Section 10.)
  2. Governance / corruption control — the trust anchor and the most disqualifying single factor; no country in the panel reached high income from below the corruption threshold.
  3. Rule of law / contract enforcement — what anchors long-horizon FDI.
  4. Cost-competitiveness / export-manufacturing — cheap-but-capable, rising world-export share, climbing the value chain.

The remaining seven are fuel, - they accelerate a country that has cleared the gates but cannot substitute for one that has not: innovation and technology adoption, investment and savings (capital formation), the demographic-dividend window, macro and monetary stability, trade openness and integration, developmental (not extractive) political continuity — which itself enters multiplicatively with governance, so that continuity rewards a clean state and penalizes a stable kleptocracy — and infrastructure quality.

Excluded: Current GDP level, share of world GDP, financial-center depth, reserve-currency usage, and military strength are excluded as predictors: under the snapshot-minus-five rule they are lagging or coincident. They identify who already rose, - the wrong signal for a convergence trade; and we track them only as outcomes to validate against.

A.Singapore as the cleanest exemplar

Among the seven positives, Singapore is the cleanest run of the play, which is why it anchors the intuition — not because the factors were derived from it. Every Singapore detail maps onto a factor that already cleared the test independently. The Economic Development Board courted multinational factories into an export-oriented economy (competitiveness gate). The Corrupt Practices Investigation Bureau made clean, predictable administration the country’s core comparative advantage and the reason global capital trusted it with long-horizon commitments (governance and rule-of-law gates). The Central Provident Fund forced national savings above 40% of GDP to self-fund the build-out (capital-formation fuel). English-medium technical and vocational education skilled the whole population, not an elite (human-capital gate). A deep-water port and first-world infrastructure plugged the island into world trade (infrastructure and trade fuel). Single-party continuity gave the twenty-five year horizon a miracle requires — but paired with meritocracy and clean governance, which is what makes it developmental rather than extractive (continuity fuel, interacted with the governance gate). Between 1965 and 1990, GDP per capita rose from roughly $500 to roughly $12,000. Singapore is the exemplar precisely because it lit up every validated factor at once.

V.Composite construction and scope

The composite mirrors the factor structure: the four leading institutional gates enter multiplicatively, so a near-floor reading on any one collapses the score; the seven fuel factors are additive. Weights are not hand-set — they are tuned by ridge/logit regression on the takeoff-versus-control panel under leave-one-takeoff-out cross-validation, so the fit never sees the case it is asked to rank. Each candidate’s composite is reported with bootstrap confidence bands over the weights, and any country whose rank flips under reasonable reweighting is flagged low-confidence.

One scope statement, stated plainly because rigor demands it. With seven labeled positives, validation is established at the factor level — each indicator is shown to generalize across the seven under leave-one-out — rather than as a calibrated per-country probability. The composite is therefore a ranking instrument, not a probability forecast. The live numbers that follow are factor readings taken from the latest published indicator values (World Bank WDI and Human Capital Index, Transparency International CPI, the World Justice Project, UN population data) scored against the framework’s validated thresholds.

VI.The candidate field

Scoring the unprejudiced field against the validated gates yields the following. We scored fourteen developing economies across Asia, Africa, and the Middle East — plus three reference cases (Thailand, Turkey, China) that illustrate the ways an ascent fails or matures. Table I reports the gate readings from the latest data.

The pattern is stark. Most candidates fail at least one gate, and the failures cluster on the institutional factors precisely as the test predicts they should. Several economies with genuinely strong fuel — Cambodia (trade at 143% of GDP, savings  34%, FDI  9%), Bangladesh (the world’s 2 garment exporter, investment  31% of GDP), the Philippines (a young population and a world-leading high-tech export share) — are nonetheless capped because their governance, human-capital, or rule-of-law readings sit near the floor. Strong demand-side numbers do not redeem a closed institutional gate.

VII.The verdict
A.Vietnam clears every gate

Vietnam is the only candidate in the field that clears all four leading gates simultaneously. Its Corruption Perceptions Index crossed the 40 line in 2024 and rose again to 41 in 2025. - the first sustained improvement after years of stagnation, on the back of an aggressive anti-corruption drive. Its Human Capital Index is 0.69, above the East-Asia-and-Pacific average and top-five within its income group; it skills the broad population, not merely an elite. It runs an unambiguously export-manufacturing-led economy: two-thirds of inbound FDI goes to manufacturing, exports run at roughly 165% of GDP, and it is gaining real world-export share as the prime beneficiary of “China + 1” supply-chain migration. And it does all this on East-Asian-miracle fuel: gross savings  35% of GDP funding  30% investment, FDI  4% of GDP into skill-building factories, sound  3% inflation, and single-party developmental continuity (continuity paired with rising governance, which reads as developmental rather than extractive). At  $4k real GDP per capita (constant 2015 US$;  $4.7k nominal) it sits in the same early-stage band Singapore and Korea occupied mid-journey, with decades of runway. Its composite is 74, - the only score in the field clearing all four gates on full fuel. That resilience is not theoretical: Vietnam was the only economy in Asia to avoid recession through the 2020 crisis, posting positive GDP growth while every regional peer contracted.

Vietnam’s weaknesses are real but second-order: rule of law outside the FDI zones is still mid-tier (CPI 41 versus Singapore’s 84), the demographic window is maturing faster than Africa’s, growth leans on US demand, and the move from assembly to indigenous innovation is unproven. These cap the magnitude of the thesis, not its direction.

B.Why the obvious comparators fall short

India is the natural runner-up and a formidable secular story: a continental open demographic window,  30% investment, the deepest capital markets and most credible indigenous innovation base of any candidate, and a rule-of-law floor above its peers. But it fails two gates: average human capital is weak (HCI 0.49, - world-class at the top, thin on average), and it is not an FDI magnet (net inflows just 0.71% of GDP). India runs a “strategic openness,” services-and-tech model — closer to China-2000s selective openness than to the Singapore export-manufacturing play — and lands at a composite of 63, the gate-incomplete runner-up.

The three reference cases sharpen the point by showing how the play fails:

  • Thailand had the early manufacturing lead and even has better rule of law than Vietnam today (WJP 0.50 vs 0.49); yet it is the textbook middle-income trap: corruption falling, human-capital quality plateaued, and a demographic window that slammed shut (fertility 1.21, a super-aged society by 2033) before it got rich.
  • Turkey had Singapore-grade human capital (HCI 0.65) and a deep, 93%-manufactures export base… and reversed the ascent by wrecking rule of law (WJP 0.42, falling) and sound money ( 65% inflation, an 80% currency collapse): Dalio’s decline phase in real time.
  • China is the template that already ran: the 1980–2010 Singapore play at continental scale; now at the “top,” aging (working-age population peaked in 2015). Everyone is ready to throw money at opportunities with potential to follow it; bringing China+1 thesis to the forefront.
VIII.Contrarian validation and conclusion

The strongest evidence that the screen tracks signal rather than consensus is that it surfaces a genuine contrarian — exactly as Dalio warns the best setups do. Rwanda has, by a wide margin, the best governance and anti-corruption of any economy in its income class: a Corruption Perceptions Index of 57 — higher than Vietnam’s, third in Sub-Saharan Africa, 43rd globally — fast predictable administration, investment-friendly law, and an explicit human-capital-and-innovation-first Vision 2050 executed with credibility. On the leading factors that actually predict ascent, a $1,070-per-capita landlocked country scores like one several tiers above it. It is capped not by institutions but by scale, landlocked geography, and a still-tiny industrial and capital base, - “Singapore governance without Singapore geography,” composite 49. We were elated to find countries so far outside of the established consensus in our readings , - as it’s a sign of the signal being idiosyncratic.

The field resolves cleanly. The economy whose current configuration most closely rhymes with the validated takeoff template is Vietnam, - the only candidate to clear all four leading gates while running East-Asian-miracle fuel, and the live beneficiary of the same China+1 migration that the incumbent’s maturation sets in motion. India is the strong but gate-incomplete runner-up; Rwanda is the contrarian that validates the method; Thailand, Turkey, and China mark the ways the play closes, reverses, or completes.

With the ranking resolved, the live question is no longer which economy but how to hold it: the section that follows turns this conclusion into a portfolio allocation built to capture the convergence run while bounding its second-order risks.

IX.Portfolio construction

The convergence thesis is a position on Vietnam; the construction question is the wrapper. The same growth can be held as Vietnamese businesses: listed equity, private equity; or as Vietnamese land and the structures on it. Both wrappers carry the same country exposure and, to first order, the same convergence return. The choice between them is not a preference for one asset class but a threshold: how much, and with what certainty, the business wrapper must out-earn the land wrapper to justify the volatility it adds.

A.The trade-off, and how to count it

The equity wrapper is the more volatile expression and carries a left tail that arrives with global risk-off, - a  43% VN-Index drawdown in 2022, realized when capital is least replaceable. Together with the governance and fraud risk of a frontier small-cap market. The land wrapper marks at lower volatility and shallower drawdowns, but is illiquid to exit, title-constrained (50-year leasehold for foreign holders), and reported off an asking/ appraisal price series that overstates realized returns. Neither dominates. Fig. 5 shows both, inflation-adjusted and year by year.

B.Da Nang, 2019–2024

The land wrapper’s realized case. Over five years Da Nang converted from a domestic beach city to an international destination: Lien Chieu port and free-trade-zone buildout, a digital-nomad inflow (+107% in 2023, clustered in An Thuong / My An), Korean and Japanese long-term tenancy. Gross yields held in the 3–4% band (beachside studios 5.9–6.1%) while prices rose, - the signature of rent rising with capital value rather than yield compression, i.e. end-user demand rather than speculative bid. Well-located assets compounded rent severalfold: one beachside house held before fund inception saw its rent rise roughly sixfold across the window, with capital value rising in step. What paid was land and well-located residential; the condotel format did not, - it oversupplied into 1.5–4.5% yields and unresolved title.

C.Quy Nhon against Da Nang-then

Quy Nhon today rhymes with Da Nang of  2019 on what sets a starting line: a low base ( 30–40% of Da Nang’s price per m²), a funded infrastructure stack, beach geography, a tourism inflection. It differs on the two things that decide whether the rhyme completes.

First, the demand mix. Da Nang already had international air access and a forming foreign-resident cluster in 2019; its inflection carried a foreign-demand component from the start. Quy Nhon’s tourism is  99% domestic and short-stay — short-term-rental occupancy 21% against Da Nang’s 61% — so the foreign rental demand that compounded Da Nang rents is absent and is precisely what the catalysts must build. Second, the anchor. Da Nang’s engine was tourism and services; Quy Nhon’s distinctive catalyst is the Becamex–VSIP industrial park, a more durable and less cyclical residential-demand source than tourism if it leases up, but early (7 tenants, $225M of a $2B target).

These differences move the asset-class weights off the Da Nang-realized mix. With foreign rental demand unproven, the yield-play residential that compounded in Da Nang is premature here; weight goes to land — which captures the convergence at the lowest carrying cost and with no leasehold decay — and to pre-construction convexity, both gated on the industrial lease-up and the airport’s international licensing rather than assumed against them. The condotel format is excluded outright: ≤50-year leasehold, no freehold title, and a realized absorption record of 1–2% in the Nha Trang / Phu Quoc analogs.

D.Composition with allocator factors

Against the factors an allocator likely already has exposure to, the land sleeve loads low by construction. Measured correlation of direct residential real estate to local equities is  0.1–0.2 (the US NCREIF reference to the S&P 500 is 0.06); de-smoothed, the honest figure is  0.35 to local equities and lower to global ones, near −0.1 to US duration, and  0 to momentum/trend and carry. The drivers — Vietnamese urbanization, industrial lease-up, a domestic tourism cycle — are not what move those factors.

X.Appendix: methodology detail

This appendix holds the reproducibility detail kept out of the body, for a reader doing diligence. The canonical source is the research methodology specification (src/README.md); what follows is its essence.

A.Per-factor data series and thresholds

Each factor is computed from named series and a published “ready” threshold.

  • Education — World Bank HCI; WDI tertiary enrollment (SE.TER.ENRR), upper-secondary completion (SE.SEC.CUAT.UP.ZS), literacy (SE.ADT.LITR.ZS); OECD PISA; Barro-Lee attainment for pre-1990 coverage. Ready: HCI ≥ 0.60, or tertiary enrollment >15% and rising with secondary completion >60% and literacy >90%; harmonized test scores below  400 cap the factor regardless of enrollment.
  • Governance / corruption — Transparency International CPI (0–100); World Bank WGI control-of-corruption (CC.EST), rule of law (RL.EST), government effectiveness (GE.EST); V-Dem v2x_rule for pre-1996 windows. Ready: CPI ≥ 40 (or WGI percentile ≥ 50th) and rising; CPI < 30 is a near-automatic disqualifier.
  • Rule of law — World Justice Project Rule of Law Index (0–1, with civil-justice and regulatory-enforcement sub-scores); WGI RL.EST; V-Dem v2x_rule, v2xnp_client. Ready: WJP ≥ 0.50 (civil-justice ≥ 0.50) or WGI percentile ≥ 50th, improving; WJP < 0.45 is a gate failure.
  • Cost-competitiveness — WDI exports (NE.EXP.GNFS.ZS), manufactures share (TX.VAL.MANF.ZS.UN), REER (PX.REX.REER), world merchandise-export share (TX.VAL.MRCH.CD.WT); WEF/IMD rankings. Ready: manufactures > 50% of merchandise exports (or rising fast) and rising world-export share at a competitive REER.
  • Innovation — WDI R&D/GDP (GB.XPD.RSDV.GD.ZS), researchers (SP.POP.SCIE.RD.P6), resident patents (IP.PAT.RESD), high-tech exports (TX.VAL.TECH.MF.ZS); WIPO GII; PWT rtfpna. Ready: R&D/GDP > 0.5% and rising; high-tech exports >10% of manufactured exports and climbing; resident patents growing >15%/yr. Scored on growth, not level.
  • Investment / savings — WDI gross fixed capital formation (NE.GDI.FTOT.ZS), gross savings (NY.GDS.TOTL.ZS), FDI (BX.KLT.DINV.WD.GD.ZS); PWT csh_i. Ready: GFCF > 25% and domestic savings > 25% of GDP, sustained, with ICOR <  4 (the quality filter separating productive Korea from bubble Cambodia).
  • Demographics — WDI age-dependency (SP.POP.DPND), working-age share (SP.POP.1564.TO.ZS), fertility (SP.DYN.TFRT.IN); UN WPP. Ready: age-dependency < 60 and falling, fertility 1.8–3.0, ≥15 years of window runway.
  • Macro stability — WDI inflation (FP.CPI.TOTL.ZG), government debt (GC.DOD.TOTL.GD.ZS), current account (BN.CAB.XOKA.GD.ZS), reserves (FI.RES.TOTL.MO); IMF WEO. Ready: inflation < 8% and low-volatility, public debt < 60% and stable/falling, reserves > 3 months of imports.
  • Trade openness — WDI trade/GDP (NE.TRD.GNFS.ZS), logistics (LP.LPI.OVRL.XQ); KOF trade sub-index; WTO RTA database. Ready: trade > 70% of GDP and rising, FTA coverage, LPI > 3.0, backed by manufactures not transit.
  • Political continuity — WGI political stability (PV.EST); V-Dem v2x_polyarchy, v2x_libdem; Polity5 durability. Ready: stability percentile > 50th and stable, paired with passing governance (CPI ≥ 40); continuity with CPI < 30 is scored as an extractive trap.
  • Infrastructure — WDI LPI (LP.LPI.OVRL.XQ), electricity access (EG.ELC.ACCS.ZS); WEF infrastructure pillar. Ready: LPI > 3.0 and rising, electricity access > 90%.
B.Coverage splicing

The modern WDI/CPI/WGI series postdate the 1962–65 Asian takeoffs. For those windows the backtest spine uses Barro-Lee (education), V-Dem v2x_rule (governance), and the Penn World Table (investment, TFP), each calibrated to the modern series on their period of overlap so that the spliced and native readings share a scale. Without this calibration the oldest and most informative templates — Korea, Taiwan, Singapore — would be unscoreable.

C.Weighting and validation

Weights are tuned by ridge/logit regression on the takeoff-versus-control panel under leave-one-takeoff-out cross-validation, so the model never fits the case it predicts. Each factor is retained only on clearing all three bars (Section 4): leave-one-out hit-rate ≥ 70%, ROC-AUC ≥ 0.70, Spearman ≥ 0.40; factors passing AUC but failing hit-rate are kept as modifiers, not gates. Composites are reported with bootstrap confidence bands over the weights, and any rank that flips under reasonable reweighting is flagged low-confidence. With seven labeled positives, validation holds at the factor level rather than as a calibrated per-country probability, - the composite is a ranking instrument.
