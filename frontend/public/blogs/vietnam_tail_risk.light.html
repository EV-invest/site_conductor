RISK

Vietnam Tail Analysis

Vietnam ran a governance improvement of  10 CPI points between 2012 and 2025 — from 31 to 41 — without a single year of growth below 5% outside of COVID. For a frontier market, that pairing is statistically improbable. The anti-corruption campaign was assumed, by the usual playbook, to carry a growth cost. It did not. This report quantifies how unusual that is, what is driving it, and which tails can still break it.

The governance-growth decoupling

Vietnam’s Transparency International CPI sat at 31 from 2012 through 2015 — a floor it held for four consecutive years. It began rising in 2016 with the launch of the anti-corruption campaign (“đốt lò” — burning the furnace) and reached 41 by 2025. Over the same window, real GDP growth averaged 6.5%, never dipping below 5% except for the 2020–2021 COVID dislocation. The simple correlation between the two series is near zero (Pearson r = 0.12), which is exactly the point: the relationship most would expect — governance crackdown slows growth — did not materialize.

Pre-2016 (2012–2016): average CPI 31.4, average growth 5.9%. Post-2016 (2016–2025, ex-COVID): average CPI 37.8, average growth 6.9%. +6 CPI points, +1.0 percentage point of GDP growth — directionally the opposite of what the literature on anti-corruption shocks predicts.

The academic consensus on governance–growth causality is that it is weak-to-nonexistent in the short run and modestly positive in the long run, with effects strongly conditional on baseline institutional quality. A 2024 SAARC panel found no Granger causality between governance and GDP growth. A 2025 spatial Durbin study of 56 developing economies found governance spillovers across borders but limited direct within-country effects. The unambiguous finding is that anti-corruption drives usually impose a short-term compliance cost — bureaucratic paralysis, investment hesitation, real-estate stagnation — before any growth dividend emerges.

Vietnam skipped the cost.

The improbable timing

The anti-corruption campaign did not lift growth by itself. It landed in a growth environment that was already running hot from an external catalyst: the China+1 supply-chain migration, which redirected manufacturing FDI into Vietnam at a scale that masked whatever domestic drag the campaign created.

Chinese FDI into Vietnam rose from  $2.5B in 2022 to $4.73B in 2024, with Q1 2025 up 68.5% year-on-year. Total FDI hit  $38B in 2024, the highest on record. The US trade deficit with Vietnam reached $124B — third-largest globally, behind China and Mexico — with  30% of Vietnam’s GDP now in exports to the US alone. The FDI was not a reward for cleaner governance. It was a supply-chain re-routing that was already underway, catalysed by US tariffs on Chinese goods from 2018 onward.

But the governance improvement compounded the FDI: multinational capital prefers predictable, clean administration, and the anti-corruption campaign delivered exactly that — a reduction in uncertainty at the transactional level that made Vietnam the lowest-friction destination in the China+1 set. A 2025 ISEAS survey found that foreign investors in Vietnam ranked corruption as a declining deterrent for the first time since the survey began. The anti-corruption campaign was not the engine — but it removed the brake.

The four tails

The decoupling holds only as long as its two pillars hold: FDI continues to flow, and the governance improvements are not reversed or overwhelmed. Four distinct scenarios can break one or both. The table below reports each tail’s expected GDP impact and its 95th-percentile drawdown, estimated from the academic and market sources cited. The expected values are probability-weighted; the tails are the worst plausible case within a 10-year hold.

US tariffs — the most probable, the shallowest

Vietnam runs a $124B trade surplus with the US. A 46% reciprocal tariff was briefly announced in April 2025 before being negotiated down, and the threat has not dissipated. The Goldman Sachs rule-of-thumb — every 10pp tariff reduces Vietnam’s GDP by  1% — is the standard first-order estimate. But it overstates the domestic hit in two ways. First, FDI firms account for over 50% of Vietnam’s US-bound exports, and their profit repatriation means a portion of the tariff incidence falls on foreign shareholders, not the domestic economy. Second, the domestic firms that do export to the US ( 22% share) have partial substitution into EU and CPTPP markets where Vietnam’s market share is still in low single digits.

The expected hit of −0.6pp GDP embeds a 50% probability of a negotiated  5% tariff, a 30% chance of 10%, a 15% chance of 25%, and a 5% chance of the 46% reciprocal rate returning. Even the 95th-percentile tail — the 46% rate, full incidence on domestic exporters — comes to roughly −2.5pp GDP. This is painful but not thesis-breaking for a country compounding at 6–7%. The deeper risk is indirect: a tariff-induced FDI slowdown, which would remove the engine itself. That is the scenario to watch.

Party succession — the unhedgeable risk

The 14th Party Congress convenes in early 2026. To Lam’s consolidation of the General Secretary role after Nguyen Phu Trong’s death in July 2024 has been rapid, but the ISEAS literature documents intensifying factional competition between the security-state conservatives and the reformist/pragmatist wing. The University of Navarra’s May 2025 political risk assessment rates sustained legitimacy as the most likely outcome (++ on internal party stability), but notes that a growth shock — particularly a US tariff shock — could convert factional tension into a delegation crisis.

The expected −3.5pp GDP hit embeds a 65% chance of continuity with minor friction, a 25% chance of stagnation-driven reform pressure (−4pp to −8pp), an 8% chance of a legitimacy crisis (−8pp to −15pp), and a 2% chance of regime fragmentation. The tail is severe precisely because it is unhedgeable: public markets cannot absorb a Vietnamese political shock, and even the land wrapper — which substitutes for equities in normal risk-off — would not clear in a succession crisis.

Mekong Delta — the gradual tail

Vietnam’s AFD/GEMMES climate macro model projects a 1.8% GDP loss at +1°C warming and 4.5% at +1.5°C. The Mekong Delta, which produces 90% of Vietnam’s rice exports and sits ≤1m above sea level, is the concentrated exposure — 47% of the delta could be submerged under a 100cm sea-level rise scenario by 2100. The annual GDP drag through 2050 is 0.02–0.10pp, which is negligible in any single year but compounds over a multi-decade hold. The tail is a major cyclone hitting the delta directly during the investment window — a −2pp to −5pp one-time hit — which carries  10% probability over a 10-year horizon per the historical return period of delta-striking storms.

South China Sea — the cheapest tail to buy

The grey-zone conflict is already live. The August 2025 Scarborough Shoal collision between Chinese and Philippine vessels is the template: not war, but enough friction to double war risk insurance premiums in the South China Sea corridor. A full-scale China–Vietnam maritime conflict carries  20% probability over the 10-year horizon by the scenarios constructed here — lower than the  30% sometimes cited in popular risk commentary, because the economic interdependence between China and Vietnam (the latter runs a  $60B trade deficit with the former, largely in intermediate inputs for re-export) acts as a mutual deterrent.

The expected −3.9pp GDP hit is the largest of the four tails, dominated by the 6% probability assigned to a full-scale conflict (−10pp to −20pp). But it is the cheapest to hedge: gold, VND put options, and geographic diversification of assets away from coastal provinces closest to the Paracel/Spratly flashpoints. A simple 10% gold overlay in an otherwise Vietnam-concentrated book cuts the SCS tail exposure roughly in half at an annual carry cost of  2% (gold storage/insurance).

How the tails stack

The four tails are not additive — they have different time profiles, and some are positively correlated (a US tariff shock raises the probability of a Party delegation crisis; a SCS incident reduces the likelihood of climate cooperation). But the structure is clear: the two deep tails (Party succession and SCS) are the lowest-probability and the hardest-to-impossible to hedge. The two shallow tails (tariffs and climate) are higher-probability but more manageable and gradual.

The investment implication is not that the tails doom the thesis. It is that a Vietnam position that does not explicitly budget for them is over-concentrated on the central case — a governance-upgrade, FDI-funded convergence run — without pricing the ways it can break. The expected combined drag of  3–5pp GDP over a decade, from all four tails together under a reasonable dependence structure, reduces a 7% CAGR thesis to roughly 6.5–6.7% real — still a compelling number, but one that accounts for the cost of the tails rather than ignoring them.
