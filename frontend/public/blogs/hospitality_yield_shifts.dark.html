ASSET FORMAT

Post-Pandemic Hospitality Yield Shifts

The post-pandemic traveller wants a private pool and four walls that are theirs alone. The question is whether the developer gets paid for giving it to them. We modelled a low-density luxury villa estate against a branded hotel tower in Quy Nhon at today’s land prices — and the 10× land-intensity handicap buries the ADR premium. The format pencils as a branded-residence sell-down, not a hold-for-yield hotel. The two must stay separable.

The demand shift

The private-villa segment compounds at  9.5% globally (GMInsights 2024, MarketIntelo, FutureDataStats) against luxury hotels at  4.5% (Orbis 2019–25, IMARC, Allied). Virtuoso’s 2026 Luxe Report records 45% of 2,400 advisors seeing a surge in exclusive-use requests; 67% forecast more travel, 55% higher per-trip spend. The shift is structural — multi-generational, longer single-location stays, privacy as luxury — and durable at roughly 70% permanence (the US 2019-to-2023 STR level-shift to  130% of 2019 held). The growth-rate spike in 2021–22 has mean-reverted; the level held.

A format that earns a  40% ADR premium and costs 3–4× more to build and land per key does not mechanically produce a higher yield. The question is arithmetic, and it demands an answer that separates the three levers that are commonly conflated: (1) the structural demand shift and its ADR translation, which is real; (2) the capital-value premium on for-sale branded residences ( 25–40%), which is a sell-down return, not a rental return; and (3) the operating yield-on-cost of the completed format, which carries the full land and build-cost weight and never recovers it from room rates alone.

This note isolates all three, models the cash-on-cash yield gap between a villa estate and a luxury hotel tower in Quy Nhon at today’s land prices, and shows which scenarios flip which levers.

The Shapley bridge above decomposes the villa-minus-hotel YoC gap into its ten constituent levers, each isolated as the marginal contribution it adds over an arbitrary ordering. Three findings:

  1. Land intensity is the whole game. At villa  1,200 m²/key against hotel  100 m²/key (Savills 2025; Zannier / InterContinental Vietnam property records), land-per-key runs  10×. Even on Quy Nhon’s $960/m², that absorbs −5.7pp of yield — the entire ADR premium and then some.

  2. ADR premium is real but insufficient. The ADR delta is +5.5pp in isolation: $630 villa against $175 hotel (Anantara Quy Nhon at  $650 vs Avani at  $155 provides the in-market reference; Regent Phu Quoc’s villa-over-suite spread of +52% provides the ceiling). It is the largest positive lever and it still loses to land intensity by roughly the width of the occupancy drag.

  3. Occupancy drag is secondary but persistent. Villa estates stabilize  56% occupancy against hotels at  68% — a −12pp gap from thinner booking funnels, owner-use blocking, and a  99% domestic demand pool with no winter international base (Bali proxy, Airbtics 2025; CBRE Vietnam H1-2025; Quy Nhon tourism statistics). The RevPAR ratio (villa / hotel) runs roughly 1.11× at central estimates — positive, but not positive enough.

The remaining levers — revenue mix, operating margin, management and incentive fees, FF&E reserve, tax, build cost — collectively contribute under ±1pp to the gap, centering near zero. The Shapley is stable: the gap is negative at the 80th percentile, and the probability villa out-yields hotel on pure cash-on-cash is  10%.

Land decides it

The land sweep shows the gap across the full land-cost axis, with the 90% confidence band from the same Monte Carlo. The gap crosses zero at roughly $375/m² — a land price that is below every published Vietnamese coastal market, including Quy Nhon. At Quy Nhon’s observed $960–1,120/m², the central gap is −1.5pp and the 90% CI runs [−3.6, +0.5]. At Da Nang’s $3,200/m², the gap is −1.6pp and the CI is entirely negative. The villa format does not out-yield a hotel tower on rental in any coastal market we can measure.

How the format actually pencils

None of this means a villa estate is a bad investment. It means it is a sell-down investment, not a yield investment, and the two have to be modelled separately.

The developer buys land cheap, builds villas, sells them as branded residences at a +25–40% capital premium (Savills 2025/26 global average +33%, resort-location +39%; Knight Frank 2025, 20–35% range) to HNW buyers who first experienced the brand as hotel guests (75% of branded-residence buyers, Knight Frank). The buyer holds for capital appreciation and personal use; the developer recycles capital at a lower exit cap rate on the sold-down units. The rental yield on the unsold inventory is a cost of carry during sell-down, not the return.

Our model’s exit-cap-rate sweep (not shown; available in the data) confirms the breakeven sensitivity: the villa YoC from a pure-rental hold clears 8% only at a sub-$400/m² land cost, holding all other parameters at their optimistic edge. The sell-down path, in contrast, embeds a +33% capital premium and amortizes land across sold strata — a structure that can work above $600/m². The two paths should never be conflated — the operating yield and the sell-down capital premium are distinct return streams with distinct risk profiles, and a model that merges them will overstate both.

Quy Nhon specifically

The local evidence is thin. Anantara Quy Nhon runs 25 pool villas at  $650/night with a 4.8 TripAdvisor rating — the format exists and commands a premium, but guest reviews describe a “quiet, not full” resort and the operator is Minor Hotels, not a luxury-villa specialist. The Avani Quy Nhon (same beach, same operator) runs hotel rooms at  $155. The FLC Quy Nhon complex (327–1,500 rooms) trades at $38–58/night for hotel and $81–163/night for resort villas — with “ghost town” occupancy and a distressed parent (chairman sentenced, assets −40%). The pool of comps is two properties and a distressed third; any claim made about Quy Nhon villa economics from just Anantara vs Avani is two data points.

The harsher binding constraint is Phu Cat Airport, which remains domestic-only as of mid-2026 — a second runway is arriving but no international license has been granted. Quy Nhon’s  99% domestic,  21% STR-occupancy demand pool does not support the stabilized occupancy or ADR a villa development’s cost structure requires. Until Phu Cat wins scheduled international service (post-2026 binary,  50–60% probability by 2028), our model’s occupancy haircut stays in place and the villa YoC stays below 3%.

Verdict

The post-pandemic demand shift to private-space luxury is real. The ADR premium for a low-density villa over a hotel suite is real — roughly +40% (in-market: Anantara Quy Nhon  $650 vs Avani  $155; Regent Phu Quoc villa-over-suite +52%). The Shapley is unambiguous: land intensity consumes all of it on a cash-on-cash basis, and the format out-yields a hotel tower only at sub-$375/m² land — cheaper than any Vietnamese coastal market.

The format does not produce a superior rental yield. It produces a superior capital event: the developer buys cheap land, builds villas, sells them at a +25–40% branded-residence premium to HNW buyers, and recycles capital. This thesis lives or dies on Phu Cat going international, the VSIP lease-up converting to HNW presence, and a developer with the brand to command the premium — none of which exist in Quy Nhon today. The bet is a convergence bet dressed in asset-format language. It is fine to take it on those terms; it is not fine to underwrite it as a hold-for-yield hotel.
