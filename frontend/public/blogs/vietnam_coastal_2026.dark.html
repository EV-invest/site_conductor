Vietnam Coastal Macro Report 2026

Vietnam’s coastal secondary cities are outperforming the saturated primary markets, driven by domestic wealth expansion and direct FDI. The live question is not whether to hold the convergence, but where it has not yet been priced.

Ho Chi Minh City and Hanoi have already done the easy part of the trade. Price-to-income in HCMC is  34 years — fifth-worst on the planet — land is scarce, and the regulatory and lending screws are tightening on exactly the leverage that inflated it. The marginal dollar of institutional real-estate capital is no longer competing for a downtown HCMC plot; it is relocating to the coast, to second-tier cities where a funded infrastructure stack is arriving before the price has caught up to it. That migration is the whole report. The only decision inside it is which coastal city you are early to, and which one you are late to.

Da Nang already ran

Da Nang is the realized case, and the reason it is worth studying is that it is now finished as an entry. Between 2019 and 2024 it converted from a domestic beach town into an international destination: the Lien Chieu deep-water port and free-trade-zone buildout, a digital-nomad inflow that grew +107% in 2023, Korean and Japanese long-term tenancy. The tell that this was real end-user demand and not a speculative bid: gross yields held in the 3–4% band while prices rose — rent climbing in step with capital value, not yield compressing under a wall of flippers. Well-located land and residential compounded several-fold.

The problem is that everyone can now see it. Da Nang apartments run  $3,200/m², up +12% year-on-year into Q1-2026, against a pipeline that is  13,000 units of incoming supply and effectively 100% luxury. At a 3.5–4.5% yield against a 12–14% floating mortgage, the levered buyer is underwater on carry from day one and betting purely on further appreciation — into overhang. Da Nang is buying the convergence after it happened. That is not a thesis; it is a momentum trade with negative running yield.

Quy Nhon is Da Nang, circa 2019

Quy Nhon (administratively folded into Gia Lai province as of 1 July 2025) trades at roughly 30–40% of Da Nang’s price per m² — apartments  $960, land  $1,120 — and it sits on a catalyst stack that is mostly funded rather than announced:

  • The Hoai Nhon–Quy Nhon expressway went operational in April 2026, pulling the city onto the continuous Hanoi–HCMC expressway spine. Banked.
  • Quy Nhon port was dredged to 50,000 DWT in 2025 — it took a 60,000-tonne bulk carrier at year-end — with a 150,000-tonne deep-water area planned at Phu My. Real, and recently upgraded.
  • The Becamex–VSIP industrial park is the single most important indicator: an industrial anchor is the most durable non-tourism residential-demand engine in second-tier Vietnam (the Hai Phong and Binh Duong precedent). It is early — 7 tenants, $225M committed against a $2B / 120,000-job target.
  • The Diêu Trì station on the approved North–South high-speed rail will serve the city; it breaks ground late 2026, with the usual Vietnamese-megaproject caveat that 2035 is the optimistic completion, not the base case.
  • Phu Cat airport is getting a second runway, but is not yet international — no immigration or customs, license not granted.

The demand side is inflecting on its own: 9.2M visitors in 2024 (+84% YoY), TripAdvisor’s 4 trending global destination for 2026, the only Vietnamese city on the list.

The single fact that threatens the thesis

Here is where the rhyme with Da Nang-2019 breaks, and it is the only part of this report that decides the trade. Da Nang’s 2019 inflection already carried a foreign demand component — international air access and a forming expat cluster were in place before prices ran. Quy Nhon’s tourism is  99% domestic and short-stay. Short-term-rental occupancy is  21%, against Da Nang’s  61%. The foreign rental demand that compounded Da Nang’s rents is not thin here — it is absent. The entire bet is that the catalysts build that demand inside the hold, not that it already exists. Until Phu Cat wins international routes and VSIP leases up, the running yield is  3% net and the case rests entirely on land appreciation and the price gap closing.

That is the inversion the factor chart shows: every factor that favours Quy Nhon is a supply-side or valuation advantage (cheapness, industrial anchor, supply discipline), and every factor that favours Da Nang-2018 is demand-side (international access, in-migration). You are paying a third of the price for a demand engine that has not turned over yet.

How to hold it

Quy Nhon has the widest gap in Vietnamese coastal real estate between catalyst-implied future demand and currently-priced supply — Da Nang has already closed that gap, the glutted resort markets (Nha Trang, Phu Quoc) closed it the wrong way with 1–2% condotel absorption. But the demand side of Quy Nhon’s gap may never fill: the bear case — Phu Cat fails to win international service, VSIP stalls at a handful of tenants, the domestic cycle turns on a +36%/yr real-estate-credit overhang — carries  30–35% probability and a −30% to −45% drawdown.

So the bet only survives in one shape. Unlevered, land or pre-construction only, small size, gated on VSIP lease-up. Levered, it barely clears breakeven and only on appreciation — identical in structure to the condotel trap, so do not lever it. Condotel-format is excluded outright: ≤50-year leasehold, no freehold title, and a 1–2% realized absorption record in the Nha Trang / Phu Quoc analogs. The two go/no-go triggers to watch quarterly are VSIP crossing  50% lease-up and Phu Cat landing scheduled international service. Clear both by  2028 and the demand leg flips from red and the position scales; stall both and it is a Nha Trang.
